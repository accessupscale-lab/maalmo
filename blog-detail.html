Cup Lock vs Ring Lock: How to Choose the Right Scaffolding System

Both Cup Lock and Ring Lock systems have shaped modern scaffolding — but they solve different problems on the job site. This guide unpacks the engineering, the economics and the situational fit so you can make a confident specification call for your next project.

The Short Answer

If you're scaffolding repetitive rectangular bays at scale and want to keep crew training simple, Cup Lock is hard to beat on cost and speed. If your project is dimensionally complex, structurally heavy, or has long-term reuse demands, Ring Lock typically pays back the premium in flexibility and rigidity.

1. The Connection — Why It Matters

Every scaffolding system rises and falls on its node connection. Cup Lock uses a top and bottom cup welded to the standard, which captures up to four ledger ends in a single hammer-strike. Ring Lock uses a forged rosette with eight pre-engineered slots, allowing ledgers and braces to enter at any angle.

The Cup Lock node is faster for a four-way orthogonal connection. The Ring Lock node is more flexible for non-90° geometry — radial scaffolds, angled tie-ins and cantilevered platforms all benefit from the multi-angle slot pattern.

2. Load Capacity Compared

Both systems comfortably exceed working load requirements for general construction access. Where Ring Lock pulls ahead is in heavy-duty applications: industrial shoring, refinery turnarounds and concrete formwork support. The forged rosette transfers larger axial and shear loads than the cast cup interface.

Typical safe working loads (per standard)

SystemSWL Range (kN)Typical Use
Cup Lock30 – 40 kNGeneral access, facade work
Ring Lock50 – 70 kNHeavy access, shoring, industrial

3. Speed of Assembly

For a flat rectangular run, Cup Lock crews routinely outpace Ring Lock crews by 15–20%. For an irregular geometry — radial towers, stepped facades, mixed cantilevers — the picture flips. Ring Lock's multi-angle node eliminates the swivel-clamp workarounds that slow Cup Lock crews on complex jobs.

"On a square podium, Cup Lock wins. On a curved museum facade, Ring Lock pays for itself in three lifts." — Maalmo site engineer, 2025

4. Total Cost of Ownership

Cup Lock has a lower per-tonne purchase cost and broader spare parts availability across India and the Middle East. Ring Lock costs 15–25% more upfront but carries longer service life, lower replacement rates and faster crew-handover on complex jobs.

For a contractor whose pipeline is mostly residential towers, Cup Lock has the better economics. For a contractor servicing refinery shutdowns or stadium builds, Ring Lock returns its premium.

5. Compliance & Documentation

Maalmo manufactures both systems to EN 12810 (facade scaffolds) and EN 12811 (working scaffolds) standards. Both are hot-dip galvanized to ASTM A123 / EN ISO 1461. Mill test reports and structural calculations are issued with every shipment — non-negotiable for export and oil-and-gas contracts.

So — Which One Should You Buy?

  • Choose Cup Lock if your work is mostly rectangular bays, your crew is mid-skilled, and your projects are cost-sensitive.
  • Choose Ring Lock if your work involves complex geometry, heavy loading, or long-term redeployment across varied projects.
  • Choose both if you operate at scale — the systems complement each other across project types, and our engineering team can size the split for you.
